URBZ

A journey to discover the urban diversity of the city

A project by Martina,Rendy

Bolzano is considered a bridge between North and South due to the three spoken languages (Italian, German, and Ladin) and the confluence of Italian and German-Austrian culture. Considered as a hybrid of diverse cultures and backgrounds, the districts of Bolzano are located in temporal spans in order to collocate them from their construction and annexation to the city, to their subsequent development.

 

The Casanova district

2000 - 2012

CasaNova district, the architectural project developed by the architect Siegfried Delueg was completed in 2012. This neighborhood, is part of a project by IPES Bolzano (Institute for Social Housing) to incentivize provision of rental housing for low income families.

At the basis of the project is the concept of sustainability, obtained through high energy performance, the use of renewable energy, mobility planning, water management and the creation of green spaces.

 

The Oltrisarco district

1166 – 1984

The Oltrisarco district in Bolzano begins to take on a specific conformation towards the beginning of the twentieth century, when the construction is accentuated in the area between the Isarco river and the Virgolo mountain. From being little more than an agglomeration of houses in the countryside, the programmatic construction of the industrial area desired by the Fascist regime after the annexation of South Tyrol to Italy in 1919 changes its shape and physiognomy, involving industrial development and construction of big grandi social housing complex.

Bolzano Sud nowadays is a production area with a mixture of industrial, craft and commercial activities, in which the fairground is also located. With about 13,500 inhabitants, it is the least populated and with a lower overall population density than the average city.

 

Don Bosco district

1925 – 1980

Don Bosco, born as a labouring district in the 30’s, was built to host italians migrants wanted from the Fascist regime. The annexation of South Tyrol to Italy after the WWI, brought to the necessity of the ‘Italianization’ of the area.

After fifty years, a significant population increase was recorded. This brought to the demolition of the houses called ‘Semirurali’, a complex characterised by their rural aspect, to build new high low-income houses as the Pifferi, characterised by their point similar to to the musical instrument. Today it is most inhabited by italians and new immigrant families.

 

Europa-Novacella district

1588 – 1954

Europa Novacella is the district with the smallest dimensions, less than one square km, but nevertheless more than 16,000 people live there. Consequently it is the most populous district of the city with a population density, with the vast majority of the inhabitants who are native Italian speakers.

The Augustinian friars of the convent of Novacella (Bressanone) recover the swamp in the seventeenth century and started viticulture. From the large vineyard today, nothing remains but a small garden not far from the church of Regina Pacis, built in 1960.

The current area is recent and was born in the era of the building expansion of the city of Bolzano in the early 30s wanted and implemented by the Fascist government with the intention of Italianizing South Tyrol, this area had been outlined to be the new working-class district, called the Littorio district.

 

Gries district

1185 – 1925

Gries-San Quirino (Gries-Quirein in German) used to be an independent village and municipality that was known beyond borders. With over 30,000 inhabitants, it is the most populated in the city with german origins. Due to the ‘Italianization’, Gries was integrated into the city of Bolzano in 1925

The merely rural character of this district, however, has been preserved up to present day, that is to say there are still many vineyards and ancient farms. The centre of the district is Piazza Gries, that is today a protected ensemble.

 

Centro Piani Rencio district

1180 – 1961

The Centre-Piani-Rencio zone is the second less inhabited zone of the city even if it is the more extend territorial part that include the ‘Colle’. The dichotomy of the buildings characterised from one side by the baroque style richly decorated with floral patterned, and, on the other side by the Fascist architecture of the 40s.

Some places of the city such as the train station, the museums and the ‘Victory monument’ are mirrors of the ‘Italianisation’. The emblematic Tribunali Piazza, emblem of discussion, contains the famous sentence ‘No one has the right to obey’ of Hannah Arendt contra opposed to the Fascist sentence ‘Believe, obey, fight’. Still today, this contrast symbolizes how sensitive is the argument.
